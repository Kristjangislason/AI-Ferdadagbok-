# May 3 — Into the Wild Heart of Borneo

There is something immediately humbling about the pace of a klotok. The wooden boat pushes gently against the current of the Sekonyer, and the jungle simply *opens* around us — dense, dripping, impossibly alive. I've barely had time to settle in before Yanto, our guide, raises a quiet hand.

Three orangutans on the riverbank.

I hold my breath. A lone male sits heavy and unbothered in the lower branches, but it's the mother who holds me completely still. She moves with unhurried grace along the muddy shore, and pressed against her chest, almost hidden in the folds of her rust-colored fur, is a tiny baby — eyes wide, fingers gripping. I feel something catch in my throat that I don't quite have words for.

The afternoon drifts by in the best possible way: the rhythm of the engine, the calls threading through the canopy, Yanto pointing out things I would never have noticed on my own.

By evening we're on the roof deck, and dinner is instant noodles eaten barefoot under an open sky. It sounds so simple, and it is — and it's perfect. Then the fireflies come. One at first, then dozens, blinking softly along the riverbanks like the jungle is signaling something back to the stars. I sit there long after the bowls are cleared, watching them pulse in the dark, the water sliding quietly beneath us.

I am not sure I have ever felt so far from the ordinary world, or so glad to be.

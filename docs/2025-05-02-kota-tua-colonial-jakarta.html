May 2 — Stepping Back in Time in Kota Tua

The heat hits like a wall the moment I step into Kota Tua, Jakarta's old Dutch colonial district. It doesn't let up — sweat clings to me all day, a constant, honest reminder that I'm somewhere far from home.

The streets here carry a different weight, layered with history. Faded colonial facades line the square, their grandeur weathered but still present, hinting at a past that feels both distant and uncomfortably close. I wander without any real agenda, letting the neighbourhood pull me where it wants.

I find a small warung tucked along a side street and order kopi tubruk. The coffee arrives thick and dark, grounds settling slowly at the bottom of the glass. I sit with it longer than I need to, watching the city move around me in its unhurried, chaotic rhythm.

Down at the old harbour, the air smells of salt and diesel. Weathered wooden boats bob quietly at the docks — it's the kind of scene that makes you feel like time here runs on its own terms.

On the way back, a street vendor catches my eye. I stop, browse, and walk away with a batik shirt — cheap, cheerful, patterned with deep browns and blues. I put it on right there on the street, already soaked through my old one anyway.

Today feels like a proper beginning.
